Website Accessibility

At its core, website accessibility ensures everyone is able to effectively use and interact with online content. By making websites accessible, we are able to create inclusive digital environemnts where everyone is able to access necessary information, products, and services.

Aside from being good practice, accessibility is also a legal requirement in many different countries. For instance, in the United States, the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) requires digital spaces to be accessible to those with disabilities, similar to how physical public spaces must accomodate accessibility needs. The ADA requires businesses, Educational institutions, and governemnt websites to follow certain guidelines and present accessible content.

To guide website creators, the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), created by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) were made to outline best practices for creating accessible websites. The WCAG focuses on four key concepts, they are as follows:

  • Perceivable: Content must be presented in ways users can perceive, for example providing alternative text for images.
  • Operable: All users should be able to effectively interact with and navigate the website, regardless of any assistive technology used.
  • Understandable: Information and operation of the User Interface should be clear and easy to comprehend.
  • Robust: Content should be adaptable with different assistive technologies.

Adhering to both the ADA and WCAG standards, we can guarantee our websites are inclusive and legally complient, preventing discrimination and promoting equal access.

Assistive Technology To Browse The Internet

Assistive technologies include any device or software that assists those with disibilities when interacting with digital content. This includes but is not limited to screen readers, alternative keyboards, and voice recognition software.

Asssistive Technology Industry Association: The ATiA hosts a variety of courses that cover a multitude of assistive technologies. While it looks like a lot of the courses must be paid for, they have a variety of free courses and webinairs that rotate in and out. In addition to the courses they also have links to various articles and tools that are free to view.

JAWS Screen Reader: Screen readers such as Job Access With Speech (JAWS) aids blind and visually impaired users to be able to easily navigate the internet and perform various computer tasks.

Windows Magnifier: Magnifiers make the screen larger to aid users in being able to better see words and images.

Alternative Input Devices

Alternative input devices range from keyboards with larger keys, to eye tracking systems, to switch devices! These different tools aid users in interacting with websites without the reliance on your standard mouse and keyboard setup.

AbleNet: AbleNet offers a variety of different assistive devices, such as switch devices, which allow users with limited mobility and dexterity to be able to control computers and other devices by pressing large, easily accessible buttons. They also have a variety of keyboards that come with enlarged keys and custom layouts for individuals with visual impairments.

Quadstick: This is a device that, by using your mouth, a person is able to control a PC, as well as various gaming consoles, such as the XBox, Playstation, and Nintendo consoles.

Eye Tracking: This link opens a pdf document about a study done on eye tracking. Eye tracking is the concept of allowing people to control different computer functions just by moving their eyes. Unfortunately there still needs to be significant development and improvement for this type of software to allow for more accessibility, but the concept is fascinating and I believe this could open more doors in the future.
